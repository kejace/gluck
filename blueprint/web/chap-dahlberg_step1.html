11 Dahlberg Step 1: the preliminary diffeomorphism (Phase D-B)

This chapter formalises Step 1 of Dahlberg’s proof of the plane case (theorem 143): from the mixed-sign four-vertex hypothesis we construct a preliminary orientation-preserving circle diffeomorphism \(\eta \) and positive constants \(0{\lt}a{\lt}b\) such that the reparametrised curvature \(\kappa \circ \eta \) agrees, up to a small-\(L^1\) error \(e\), with the clean bicircle \(a(1-f)+bf\). The clean bicircle is strictly positive, so all of the in-tree positive-case machinery (winding, chord-integral simplicity) applies to it; Phase D-C (chapter 12) then transports the conclusion to \(\kappa \circ \eta \) through the \(L^1\) smallness of \(e\).

Throughout, \(\kappa : \mathbb {R}\to \mathbb {R}\) is continuous and \(2\pi \)-periodic (the project’s encoding of a function on the circle \(\mathbf{T}\)), and \(f\) is the fixed two-arc indicator below. Note that, in contrast with the positive case, \(\kappa \) is not assumed positive: positivity is hypothesised only at the two maxima.

[Periodicity and integrability of \(f\) and \(k_{a,b}\)] The indicator \(f\) is \(2\pi \)-periodic and interval-integrable on every \([c,d]\); consequently the clean bicircle \(k_{a,b}=a(1-f)+bf\) is \(2\pi \)-periodic and interval-integrable, and satisfies the two-sided bound \(a\le k_{a,b}\le b\) when \(a\le b\).

Proof ▶

Periodicity and integrability of \(k_{a,b}\) follow by linear combination. 11.2 Level extraction from the four-vertex condition

In the positive case the in-tree lemma 14 (exists_abab_of_fourVertex) takes the levels \(a,b\) to be the extreme values \(\max (\kappa (q_1),\kappa (q_2))\) and \(\min (\kappa (p_1),\kappa (p_2))\) and relies on global positivity (\(a{\gt}0\) because the minima are positive). In the mixed-sign case the minima may be \(\le 0\), so that choice fails. We must instead pick the two common levels \(0{\lt}a{\lt}b\) interior to the gap \(\bigl(\max (0,\max (\kappa (q_1),\kappa (q_2))),\ \min (\kappa (p_1),\kappa (p_2))\bigr)\) — non-empty because positivity is hypothesised at the maxima and the value-separation guarantees the gap is open — and attain them on the four flanks by the intermediate value theorem. This is the only place the positivity-at-the-maxima hypothesis is used.

[Strictly positive four-vertex curvature is mixed-sign] Let \(\kappa \) be a curvature function (definition 8: continuous, \(2\pi \)-periodic and strictly positive) which is non-constant and satisfies the four-vertex condition (definition 12). Then \(\kappa \) satisfies the mixed-sign four-vertex condition (definition 114). In particular the mixed-sign hypothesis subsumes the non-constant strictly-positive case, so the mixed-sign converse applies to every non-constant positive curvature function meeting the four-vertex condition.

Proof ▶

Since \(\kappa \) is non-constant, the constant branch of the four-vertex condition is excluded, so definition 12 supplies the four value-separated alternating extrema \(p_1{\lt}q_1{\lt}p_2{\lt}q_2{\lt}p_1+2\pi \) with \(p_1,p_2\) local maxima, \(q_1,q_2\) local minima, and \(\max (\kappa (q_1),\kappa (q_2)){\lt}\min (\kappa (p_1),\kappa (p_2))\). These data, together with continuity and \(2\pi \)-periodicity, are exactly the requirements of the mixed-sign condition except for the extra positivity-at-the-maxima clause \(0{\lt}\min (\kappa (p_1),\kappa (p_2))\). But that clause is automatic: \(\kappa \) is everywhere strictly positive, so \(0{\lt}\kappa (p_1)\) and \(0{\lt}\kappa (p_2)\), whence \(0{\lt}\min (\kappa (p_1),\kappa (p_2))\). Thus \(\kappa \) satisfies definition 114.

[Mixed-sign level extraction] Source: Dahlberg, §3, “by continuity there are points \(r_j\)…”. Let \(\kappa \) satisfy the mixed-sign four-vertex condition (definition 114). Then there exist constants \(0{\lt}a{\lt}b\) and four points \(r_1{\lt}r_2{\lt}r_3{\lt}r_4{\lt}r_1+2\pi \), ordered counterclockwise, with

\[ \kappa (r_1)=\kappa (r_3)=a,\qquad \kappa (r_2)=\kappa (r_4)=b. \]
Proof ▶

Write \(m=\max (\kappa (q_1),\kappa (q_2))\) and \(M=\min (\kappa (p_1),\kappa (p_2))\); by hypothesis \(m{\lt}M\) and \(0{\lt}M\). Pick two levels with \(\max (0,m){\lt}a{\lt}b{\lt}M\) (possible since the interval \((\max (0,m),M)\) is non-empty: \(\max (0,m){\lt}M\) holds because \(m{\lt}M\) and \(0{\lt}M\)). The four flanks are the closed arcs \([p_1,q_1]\), \([q_1,p_2]\), \([p_2,q_2]\), \([q_2,p_1+2\pi ]\); on each, \(\kappa \) runs monotonically (in value, not assumed monotone — the IVT only needs the endpoint values to straddle the target) between a maximum value \(\ge M{\gt}b{\gt}a\) and a minimum value \(\le m{\lt}a{\lt}b\). Apply the intermediate value theorem lemma 11 four times:

  • on the descent \([p_1,q_1]\) pick \(r_1\) with \(\kappa (r_1)=a\) (between \(\kappa (q_1)\le m{\lt}a\) and \(\kappa (p_1)\ge M{\gt}a\));

  • on the ascent \([q_1,p_2]\) pick \(r_2\) with \(\kappa (r_2)=b\);

  • on the descent \([p_2,q_2]\) pick \(r_3\) with \(\kappa (r_3)=a\);

  • on the ascent \([q_2,p_1+2\pi ]\) pick \(r_4\) with \(\kappa (r_4)=b\) (using \(\kappa (p_1+2\pi )=\kappa (p_1)\) by periodicity).

Then \(p_1\le r_1\le q_1\le r_2\le p_2\le r_3\le q_2\le r_4\le p_1+2\pi \) gives the ordering \(r_1{\lt}r_2{\lt}r_3{\lt}r_4{\lt}r_1+2\pi \) (the inequalities are strict because the values \(a\ne b\) differ across each interior endpoint \(q_i,p_i\)), and the value pattern is \(a,b,a,b\) as required. This is the same four-fold IVT skeleton as lemma 14; only the choice of the levels \(a,b\) (interior, rather than the extreme values) and the use of maxima-positivity differ.

[Step 1: preliminary diffeomorphism] Source: Dahlberg, §3, Step 1. Let \(\kappa \) be continuous, \(2\pi \)-periodic, non-constant and satisfy the mixed-sign four-vertex condition (definition 114). There are constants \(0{\lt}a{\lt}b\) (the levels of lemma 116) and, for every \(\varepsilon {\gt}0\), a \(C^1\) (in fact \(C^\infty \)) orientation-preserving circle diffeomorphism \(\eta \) (\(\eta (t+2\pi )=\eta (t)+2\pi \), \(\eta '{\gt}0\)) and an interval-integrable, \(2\pi \)-periodic error \(e\) with

\[ \kappa \circ \eta \; =\; a(1-f) + b\, f + e, \qquad \int _0^{2\pi } |e(t)|\, dt \; {\lt}\; C\, \varepsilon , \]

where the constant \(C\) does not depend on \(\varepsilon \). Consequently the total curvature \(I=\int _0^{2\pi }\kappa \circ \eta \) satisfies \(I = (a+b)\pi + \int _0^{2\pi }e\), so \(|I-(a+b)\pi |{\lt}C\varepsilon \) and in particular \(I{\gt}0\) once \(\varepsilon \) is small (since \(a,b{\gt}0\)).

Proof ▶

Take \(a,b\) and the points \(r_j\) from lemma 116. Following Dahlberg, fix the model breakpoints \(\theta _j=(j-1)\tfrac {\pi }{2}\) and the shrunken arcs \(A_j=\{ \theta :|\theta -\theta _j|{\lt}\tfrac {\pi }{4}-\varepsilon \} \). By continuity of \(\kappa \) choose closed arcs \(B_j\ni r_j\) with \(|\kappa (w)-\kappa (r_j)|{\lt}\varepsilon \) for \(w\in B_j\). Let \(\eta \) be any smooth orientation-preserving circle diffeomorphism carrying \(A_j\) into \(B_j\) for \(j=1,\dots ,4\) (such an \(\eta \) exists: prescribe \(\eta \) on the four model arcs to land in \(B_j\) and interpolate monotonically and smoothly on the four complementary transition gaps, each of length \(2\varepsilon \)).

On the bulk \(A_j\) the composite \(\kappa \circ \eta \) is within \(\varepsilon \) of the constant \(\kappa (r_j)\), which equals the clean-bicircle value \(a\) (for \(j=1,3\)) or \(b\) (for \(j=2,4\)). Hence the error \(e=\kappa \circ \eta -(a(1-f)+bf)\) satisfies \(|e|{\lt}\varepsilon \) on \(\bigcup _j A_j\), a set of total measure \(2\pi -8\varepsilon \), contributing less than \(2\pi \varepsilon \) to \(\int |e|\). On the four transition gaps (total measure \(8\varepsilon \)) the error is bounded by \(2M\), where \(M=\sup |\kappa |+\max (a,b)\) is a fixed bound independent of \(\varepsilon \); this contributes less than \(16M\varepsilon \). Thus \(\int _0^{2\pi }|e|{\lt}(2\pi +16M)\varepsilon =:C\varepsilon \) with \(C\) independent of \(\varepsilon \).

Finally \(\int _0^{2\pi }(a(1-f)+bf)=a\cdot (2\pi -2\cdot \tfrac {\pi }{2})+b\cdot (2\cdot \tfrac {\pi }{2}) =a\pi +b\pi =(a+b)\pi \) (each value-\(b\) arc has length \(\tfrac {\pi }{2}\), two of them), so \(I=(a+b)\pi +\int e\) and the stated bound on \(I\) follows from \(|\int e|\le \int |e|{\lt}C\varepsilon \).

Proof ▶

Take \(\eta (\theta )=h_1(\theta +\tfrac {\pi }{4})\) with \(h_1(\theta )=m_0+\int _0^\theta w\), \(w\) the plateau density of \(\mathrm{exists\_ plateau\_ density}\) at the crossing points \(r_j\); the \(+\tfrac {\pi }{4}\) shift aligns the four model plateaus (centred at \(0,\tfrac {\pi }{2},\pi ,\tfrac {3\pi }{2}\)) with the constancy arcs of \(k_{a,b}\). \(\eta \) is \(C^1\) with \(v=w(\cdot +\tfrac {\pi }{4}){\gt}0\) (chain rule) and quasiperiodic (\(\eta (t+2\pi )=\eta (t)+2\pi \)). The remaining work — finding the closing adjustment over the in-tree configuration disk and transporting closure and simplicity through \(\int |e|{\lt}C\varepsilon \) — is chapter 12.
