13 The spherical converse (S², positive curvature)

This chapter formalizes the spherical converse to the four vertex theorem in the positive-curvature (Gluck-style) regime: a prescribed cyclic geodesic-curvature function on the round sphere \(S^2\), with the four-vertex property and a uniform lower bound forcing confinement to an open hemisphere, is the geodesic curvature of a simple closed curve contained in that hemisphere. The Euclidean development (chapter 7 and its dependencies, culminating in gluck_converse and dahlbergConverse) is complete and is reused, not disturbed. The positive-stage capstone is named sphericalConverse_pos; the name sphericalConverse is reserved for the mandatory mixed-sign stage 2.

All spherical geometry is transported to the stereographic disk model: the open unit disk \(\{ |z|{\lt}1\} \subset \mathbb C\) with the round metric \(g_{S^2}=\dfrac {4}{(1+|z|^2)^2}\, |dz|^2\). Stereographic projection from the missed antipode is a conformal diffeomorphism onto \(S^2\) minus a point, so a curve confined to \(\{ |z|{\lt}1\} \) is simple on \(S^2\) iff its disk representative is simple in \(\mathbb C\).

13.1 The conformal geodesic-curvature law

Write \(z=(x,y)\in \mathbb R^2\cong \mathbb C\) for a point of the disk and let a curve have unit Euclidean tangent \(T=e^{i\varphi }\) at inclination angle \(\varphi \). We use the outward (right) unit normal

\[ n=-iT=e^{i(\varphi -\pi /2)}=(\sin \varphi ,-\cos \varphi ),\qquad \langle z,n\rangle =x\sin \varphi -y\cos \varphi . \]

For the isothermal factor \(e^{2\lambda }=4/(1+|z|^2)^2\) one has \(\lambda =\ln 2-\ln (1+|z|^2)\) and \(\nabla \lambda =-2z/(1+|z|^2)\), so \(\partial _n\lambda =\langle \nabla \lambda ,n\rangle =-2\langle z,n\rangle /(1+|z|^2)\). The conformal transformation law \(\kappa _g=e^{-\lambda }(\kappa _E+\partial _n\lambda )\) gives the fundamental identity used throughout:

\[ \kappa _S \; =\; \frac{1+|z|^2}{2}\, \kappa _E \; -\; \langle z,n\rangle . \]

Here \(\kappa _E\) is the Euclidean signed curvature of the disk representative and \(\kappa _S\) its spherical geodesic curvature. In the tangent-angle gauge (section 13.2) \(\kappa _E=\varphi '/\| z'\| \), so the law rearranges to the speed relation

\[ \frac{1+|z|^2}{2}\, \varphi ' \; =\; \bigl(\kappa _S+\langle z,n\rangle \bigr)\, \| z'\| , \qquad \text{gauge speed}\quad q=\frac{1+|z|^2}{2\bigl(\kappa _S+\langle z,n\rangle \bigr)} . \]

Geodesic-circle sanity anchor (decisive sign check).

A counterclockwise Euclidean circle \(|z|=r\) has \(\kappa _E=1/r\) and outward normal \(n=z/r\), so \(\langle z,n\rangle =r\) and \(\kappa _S=\dfrac {1+r^2}{2r}-r=\dfrac {1-r^2}{2r}=\cot \rho \) with \(r=\tan (\rho /2)\) — exactly the geodesic curvature of a spherical circle of intrinsic radius \(\rho \) (and \(0\) on the equator \(\rho =\pi /2\)). The gauge speed is then \(q=r\). The opposite sign choice would give \((1+3r^2)/(2r)\ne \cot \rho \), so the outward normal with a minus sign is the correct convention; any Lean formalization must reproduce \(q(\text{circle }r)=r\).

Lean encoding of the normal term.

In Lean the left normal \(iT\) is the natural object: \(\langle z,iT\rangle =\langle z, i\, e^{i\varphi }\rangle =-x\sin \varphi +y\cos \varphi =-\langle z,n\rangle \). Hence the speed relation is encoded with \(\langle z,n\rangle \) replaced by \(-\langle z,iT\rangle \):

\[ \frac{1+\| z\| ^2}{2}\, \varphi ' =\bigl(\kappa -\langle z,\, i\, e^{i\varphi }\rangle _{\mathbb R}\bigr)\, \| z'\| . \]

The project supplies the rewrite lemma \(\langle z, i\, e^{i\varphi }\rangle _{\mathbb R}=-(\operatorname {Re}z)\sin \varphi +(\operatorname {Im}z)\cos \varphi \) so both forms are interchangeable.

13.3 Proof arc

The proof follows the Euclidean Route A architecture, with the reconstruction integral replaced by a reconstruction ODE. The architecture was pinned at iteration 046 (design analysis + external consult, load-bearing computations re-verified in-project):

  1. S2-B (truncated flow, unconditional). The gauge speed is truncated algebraically (definition 156) so the ODE field becomes globally Lipschitz and bounded; global existence on \([0,2\pi ]\), uniqueness, and continuous dependence on the initial point are then unconditional, with no confinement prerequisite. Only the closed curve found at the end must be shown, a posteriori, to be admissible (both truncations inactive), whereupon it is an honest spherical curve.

  2. S2-C (admissibility by margin transport). The confinement mechanism is perturbative: an explicit model trajectory (a spherical bicircle made of circular arcs, lemma 185) is admissible with quantitative margins, and a Grönwall estimate with \(L^1\)-in-\(\theta \) drive (lemma 178) transports the margins to every trajectory whose curvature is \(L^1\)-close and whose start is near the model start. The Dahlberg-style reparametrization already formalized in chapter 11 supplies arbitrarily small \(L^1\) distance to a two-value step model, so no uniform-in-\(\kappa _0\) direct confinement estimate is needed (none exists: no disk is flow-invariant, see the discussion in lemma 178).

  3. S2-D (endpoint map and winding). The degree argument runs over a compact \(2\)-disk of initial points \(z_0\), but its computable model is the symmetric two-value (\(\pi \)-periodic) step curvature, exploited through the half-turn equivariance \(z\mapsto -z\), \(\theta \mapsto \theta +\pi \) (lemma 188). The constant-curvature model is unusable: for constant \(\kappa \) every admissible trajectory closes (all spherical circles), so the endpoint error vanishes identically and carries no degree — see the DO-NOT recorded in lemma 211.

The speed, flow, transport, and step-model layers (S2-A through S2-D except the final assembly) are formalized axiom-clean; what remains is the assembly lemma 211, the S2-E simplicity chain, and the capstone.

13.4 Truncated flow layer (S2-B)

Design decision (iter-046). The reconstruction field is truncated algebraically — the norm clamped in the numerator, the denominator clamped from below — so that it becomes globally defined, bounded, and globally Lipschitz in \(z\). All flow machinery (existence on \([0,2\pi ]\), uniqueness, continuous dependence, the endpoint map) is then unconditional: no confinement lemma is needed to run the degree argument. Admissibility is re-imposed a posteriori, only on the single closed trajectory the winding argument produces (section 13.5). This removes the continuation/stitching bookkeeping entirely: one Picard–Lindelöf application on a large ball covers \([0,2\pi ]\).

Everything is explicit arc algebra. Because the step is piecewise constant, its model trajectories are concatenations of explicit circular arcs (lemma 185); no flow, no variational equation, and no \(\mathrm{PicardLindel\ddot of}\) package is needed on the model side. \[ W(z_0^\ast )=0,\qquad DW(z_0^\ast )=-\tfrac {2r^\ast }{1+c^2}\cdot \overline{(\, \cdot \, )} \qquad (z_0^\ast =-i\, r^\ast ,\ r^\ast =\sqrt{1+c^2}-c), \]

Transport. The comparison of the actual (reparametrized) \(\kappa \)-flow with the step model cannot use lemma 178 directly (\(\kappa ^\ast \) is discontinuous, and the concatenated model has corners), and Mathlib-style continuity of the drive fails at the breakpoints.
