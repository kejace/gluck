10 Arc-length curvature representation (Dahlberg)

This chapter opens the extension of the converse to the four-vertex theorem from the strictly positive case (Gluck 1971, theorem 87) to Dahlberg’s mixed-sign full converse, where positivity is assumed only at the two maxima. The primary source is Dahlberg, The Converse of the Four Vertex Theorem, Proc. AMS 133 (2005), 2131–2135.

Dahlberg parametrises the reconstructed curve by arc length \(s\) rather than by the inclination angle \(\theta \) used in the positive case (chapter 3). The arc-length representation is the natural setting for mixed-sign curvature: where the inclination parametrisation needs \(\kappa {\gt}0\) to make \(\theta \) a coordinate, the arc-length curve \(\gamma _K(t)=\int _0^t e^{i\alpha }\) is well defined for any continuous \(K\).

The key design decision (per the project’s unification directive) is that we do not introduce a second curvature-realization predicate. We prove that the arc-length curve realizes \(K\) through the same intrinsic predicate definition 3 already used by theorem 87, and we state the mixed-sign theorem with the same conclusion \(\exists \gamma , \mathrm{IsSimpleClosed}\, \gamma \wedge \mathrm{RealizesCurvature}\, \gamma \, \kappa \).

Throughout, \(K,\kappa : \mathbb {R}\to \mathbb {R}\) are continuous and \(2\pi \)-periodic (the project’s encoding of a function on the circle \(\mathbf{T}\)).

[The arc-length curve realizes \(K\) — unification with definition 3] For any continuous \(K : \mathbb {R}\to \mathbb {R}\) (no positivity required), the arc-length curve realizes \(K\) in the intrinsic sense:

\[ \mathrm{RealizesCurvature}\, (\gamma _K)\, (K). \]

This is the structural unification: the arc-length representation and the positive-case inclination reconstruction (lemma 88) satisfy the same predicate definition 3; there are not two parallel curvature notions.

Proof ▶

By lemma 97 \(\gamma _K\in C^1\) with \(\gamma _K'(t)=e^{i\alpha _K(t)}\neq 0\), so \(\gamma _K\) is regular. Take the tangent-angle function \(\varphi =\alpha _K\), which is differentiable with \(\varphi '(t)=\alpha _K'(t)=K(t)\). Then \(\lVert \gamma _K'(t)\rVert = \lVert e^{i\alpha _K(t)}\rVert =1\), so the tangent equation \(\gamma _K'(t)=\lVert \gamma _K'(t)\rVert \, e^{i\varphi (t)}=e^{i\alpha _K(t)}\) holds, and the curvature equation \(\varphi '(t)=K(t)=K(t)\cdot 1=K(t)\, \lVert \gamma _K'(t)\rVert \) holds. Thus \(\mathrm{RealizesCurvature}\, (\gamma _K)\, (K)\).

10.2 Closure and simplicity conditions

[Arc-length curvature conditions (1.1)–(1.3)] Source: Dahlberg, §1, conditions (1.1)–(1.3). A continuous, \(2\pi \)-periodic \(K : \mathbb {R}\to \mathbb {R}\) is an arc-length curvature function if

\begin{align} \tag {1.1} & \int _0^{2\pi } K(s)\, ds = 2\pi , \\ \tag {1.2} & \int _0^{2\pi } e^{i\alpha _K(s)}\, ds = 0 \quad \bigl(\text{equivalently } \gamma _K(2\pi )=0\bigr), \\ \tag {1.3} & \int _t^\tau e^{i\alpha _K(s)}\, ds \neq 0 \quad \text{whenever } 0 \le t {\lt} \tau {\lt} 2\pi \quad \bigl(\text{equivalently } \gamma _K(\tau )\neq \gamma _K(t)\bigr). \end{align}

Geometrically: (1.1) fixes a well-determined tangent at \(s=0\) (it forces \(e^{i\alpha _K}\) to be \(2\pi \)-periodic), (1.2) closes the curve, (1.3) makes it simple.

10.4 Reduction to a non-normalised curvature function

The previous theorem applies to \(K\) already satisfying the normalisation (1.1). The mixed-sign construction (Phases D-B, D-C) instead produces, after a reparametrisation, a function \(\kappa \circ \varphi \) that is a non-normalised curvature function: a rescaling and reparametrisation recover the theorem. This section records that reduction, which is what D-B/D-C must establish.

[Reduction: a non-normalised reparametrisation realizes \(\kappa \)] Source: Dahlberg, §1, reduction paragraph after (1.4). Let \(\kappa : \mathbb {R}\to \mathbb {R}\) be continuous and \(2\pi \)-periodic. Suppose there is a \(C^1\) orientation-preserving circle diffeomorphism \(\varphi \) (with \(C^1\) inverse \(\psi \), \(\varphi (t+2\pi )=\varphi (t)+2\pi \)) such that \(\kappa \circ \varphi \) is a non-normalised curvature function with positive total curvature \(I=\int _0^{2\pi }\kappa \circ \varphi \, dt{\gt}0\). Then

\[ \exists \, \gamma ,\ \mathrm{IsSimpleClosed}\, \gamma \, \wedge \, \mathrm{RealizesCurvature}\, \gamma \, \kappa . \]

(The hypothesis \(I{\gt}0\) is exactly what Phase D-B delivers: there \(\kappa \circ \varphi =a(1-f)+bf+e\) with \(a,b{\gt}0\) and \(e\) of small integral, so \(I\approx \int (a(1-f)+bf){\gt}0\). The bare \(I\neq 0\) of (1.4) suffices for \(K=\frac{2\pi }{I}\kappa \circ \varphi \) to satisfy (1.1); the sign is pinned by the construction.)

Proof ▶

Set \(I=\int _0^{2\pi }\kappa \circ \varphi \, dt\) (\(\neq 0\)) and \(K=\frac{2\pi }{I} \kappa \circ \varphi \), an arc-length curvature function by hypothesis and definition 105. By theorem 104 the curve \(\gamma _K\) is simple closed and realizes \(K\). Since \(I{\gt}0\) the constant \(c=\tfrac {2\pi }{I}{\gt}0\), so lemma 106 (which needs \(c{\gt}0\)) applies: \(c\, \gamma _K\) realizes \(K/c=\tfrac {I}{2\pi }K=\tfrac {I}{2\pi }\cdot \tfrac {2\pi }{I}\, \kappa \circ \varphi =\kappa \circ \varphi \), and by lemma 107 \(c\, \gamma _K\) is simple closed. Finally reparametrise by \(\psi =\varphi ^{-1}\): by lemma 89 (realization transfers under an orientation-preserving \(C^1\) reparametrisation), \((c\, \gamma _K)\circ \psi \) realizes \((\kappa \circ \varphi )\circ \psi =\kappa \circ (\varphi \circ \psi )=\kappa \), and by lemma 90 \((c\, \gamma _K)\circ \psi \) is simple closed. Take \(\gamma =(c\, \gamma _K)\circ \psi \). This is Dahlberg’s \(\Gamma (t)=\frac{2\pi }{I}\gamma _K(\psi (t))\).
