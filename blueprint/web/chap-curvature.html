2 Curvature functions on the circle

The data of the converse theorem is a curvature function preassigned on the circle \(S^1 = \mathbb {R}/2\pi \mathbb {Z}\), modelled as a \(2\pi \)-periodic function \(\kappa : \mathbb {R} \to \mathbb {R}\).

  • Gluck.IsCurvatureFunction

[Curvature function] Source: DeTurck–Gluck, §III–4 (strictly positive curvature case). A curvature function is a map \(\kappa : \mathbb {R} \to \mathbb {R}\) that is continuous, \(2\pi \)-periodic (\(\kappa (\theta + 2\pi ) = \kappa (\theta )\)), and strictly positive (\(\kappa (\theta ) {\gt} 0\) for all \(\theta \)). This is exactly the class of preassigned curvatures treated in Gluck’s positive case.

  • Gluck.radius

[Radius of curvature] Source: DeTurck–Gluck, §4 (the reconstruction integral has integrand \(1/\kappa \)). The radius of curvature of a curvature function \(\kappa \) is \(\rho = 1/\kappa \), i.e. \(\rho (\theta ) = 1/\kappa (\theta )\). It is the integrand weight \(1/\kappa (\theta )\) appearing in the source’s reconstruction formula \(\alpha (\theta ) = \int _0^\theta e^{i\theta }\, d\theta /\kappa (\theta )\).

  • Gluck.radius_pos

[Radius is a positive periodic continuous weight] If \(\kappa \) is a curvature function then \(\rho = 1/\kappa \) is continuous, \(2\pi \)-periodic and strictly positive.

Proof ▶

Continuity of \(\rho = 1/\kappa \) follows from continuity of \(\kappa \) together with \(\kappa \) being everywhere nonzero (indeed positive). Periodicity is inherited from \(\kappa \). Positivity of \(1/\kappa \) follows from positivity of \(\kappa \).

  • Gluck.ivt_hits

[Intermediate value on a closed interval, endpoints in either order] A continuous \(f : \mathbb {R} \to \mathbb {R}\) on a closed interval \([p, q]\) (with \(p \le q\)) attains every value \(v\) lying between \(f(p)\) and \(f(q)\) (in either order): if \(v \in [\min (f(p), f(q)),\ \max (f(p), f(q))]\) then there is \(x \in [p, q]\) with \(f(x) = v\).

Proof ▶

Project-bespoke packaging of the intermediate value theorem. Split on whether \(f(p) \le f(q)\) or \(f(q) \le f(p)\); in each case rewrite the \(\min /\max \) and apply the monotone, respectively anti-monotone, form of the intermediate value theorem on \([p, q]\).

  • Gluck.FourVertexCondition

[Four-vertex condition] Source: DeTurck–Gluck, Converse statement (§III). A curvature function \(\kappa \) satisfies the four-vertex condition if either

  • \(\kappa \) is constant; or

  • \(\kappa \) has two value-separated, alternating local extrema: there are four points

    \[ p_1 {\lt} q_1 {\lt} p_2 {\lt} q_2, \qquad q_2 {\lt} p_1 + 2\pi , \]

    lying in a single fundamental period, such that \(p_1, p_2\) are local maxima of \(\kappa \), \(q_1, q_2\) are local minima of \(\kappa \), and the two minimum values lie strictly below the two maximum values:

    \[ \max \bigl(\kappa (q_1), \kappa (q_2)\bigr) \; {\lt}\; \min \bigl(\kappa (p_1), \kappa (p_2)\bigr). \]

Why the strict value-separation (a project-level correctness refinement of the source’s phrasing). The source says “at least two local maxima and two local minima.” A naive formalization — “there exist two distinct points that are each a local maximum, and two distinct points that are each a local minimum” using the topological (non-strict) notion of local extremum — is too weak and makes the converse theorem false. Counterexample: a trapezoidal wave that is constant \(=1\) on a bottom plateau, rises to a top plateau where it is constant \(=2\), and falls back, is unimodal yet has infinitely many points that are non-strict local maxima (the whole top plateau) and minima (the bottom plateau). It satisfies the naive condition but has no four points carrying values \(a,b,a,b\) with \(a{\lt}b\) in cyclic order (each level is crossed exactly twice), and indeed is not realizable as a closed curve. The genuine content of “two local maxima and two local minima” is that \(\kappa \) oscillates at least twice: two separated humps whose tops strictly exceed the two intervening valleys. The condition above encodes exactly that and is what the realizability proof consumes (it is precisely equivalent, for positive curvature, to the existence of the \(a,b,a,b\) interleaving of lemma 14).

  • Gluck.exists_abab_of_fourVertex

[Four values \(a,b,a,b\) around the circle] If a non-constant curvature function \(\kappa \) satisfies the four-vertex condition, then there exist \(0 {\lt} a {\lt} b\) and four points \(\theta _1 {\lt} \theta _2 {\lt} \theta _3 {\lt} \theta _4\) lying in a single fundamental period (\(\theta _4 {\lt} \theta _1 + 2\pi \)) at which \(\kappa \) takes the values \(a, b, a, b\) in order around the circle.

Proof ▶

Source: DeTurck–Gluck, §5, p. 11 (the source asserts this; we prove it from the value-separated four-vertex condition).

Since \(\kappa \) is non-constant, the four-vertex condition gives its second disjunct: four points \(p_1 {\lt} q_1 {\lt} p_2 {\lt} q_2\) in one period, with \(q_2 {\lt} p_1 + 2\pi \), where \(p_1, p_2\) are local maxima, \(q_1, q_2\) local minima, and

\[ \max \bigl(\kappa (q_1), \kappa (q_2)\bigr) \; {\lt}\; \min \bigl(\kappa (p_1), \kappa (p_2)\bigr). \]

Step 1 (the two levels). Put

\[ a := \max \bigl(\kappa (q_1), \kappa (q_2)\bigr), \qquad b := \min \bigl(\kappa (p_1), \kappa (p_2)\bigr). \]

The value-separation hypothesis is exactly \(a {\lt} b\), and \(a {\gt} 0\) because \(\kappa {\gt} 0\) (so \(a \ge \kappa (q_1) {\gt} 0\)).

Step 2 (four sub-arcs, each spanning \([a,b]\)). Consider the four closed sub-arcs

\[ [p_1, q_1],\quad [q_1, p_2],\quad [p_2, q_2],\quad [q_2, p_1 + 2\pi ]. \]

Each joins a maximum endpoint (value \(\ge b\)) to a minimum endpoint (value \(\le a\)); for the last sub-arc the right endpoint value is \(\kappa (p_1 + 2\pi ) = \kappa (p_1) \ge b\) by periodicity. Hence on each sub-arc the interval \([a,b]\) lies between the two endpoint values, and by the intermediate value theorem (lemma 12) \(\kappa \) attains every value of \([a,b]\) on each sub-arc.

Step 3 (select the four points). Choose

\[ \theta _1 \in [p_1, q_1] \text{ with } \kappa (\theta _1) = a, \quad \theta _2 \in [q_1, p_2] \text{ with } \kappa (\theta _2) = b, \]
\[ \theta _3 \in [p_2, q_2] \text{ with } \kappa (\theta _3) = a, \quad \theta _4 \in [q_2, p_1 + 2\pi ] \text{ with } \kappa (\theta _4) = b, \]

all possible by Step 2. The endpoint inclusions give the weak chain

\[ p_1 \le \theta _1 \le q_1 \le \theta _2 \le p_2 \le \theta _3 \le q_2 \le \theta _4 \le p_1 + 2\pi . \]

The assigned values alternate \(a, b, a, b\) with \(a \ne b\), so consecutive chosen points carry different \(\kappa \)-values, hence are distinct; combined with the weak chain this upgrades it to the strict chain \(\theta _1 {\lt} \theta _2 {\lt} \theta _3 {\lt} \theta _4\).

Step 4 (the points lie in one period). Since \(\kappa (p_1) \ge b {\gt} a = \kappa (\theta _1)\) we have \(\theta _1 \ne p_1\), so \(\theta _1 {\gt} p_1\); together with \(\theta _4 \le p_1 + 2\pi \) this gives \(\theta _4 {\lt} \theta _1 + 2\pi \). Thus the four points lie in a single fundamental period and \(\kappa \) takes the values \(a, b, a, b\) on them in order around the circle, with \(0 {\lt} a {\lt} b\), as required.
