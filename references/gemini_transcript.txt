Are you familiar with the four vertex theorem?

Yes, I am familiar with the Four Vertex Theorem. It is a classic and elegant result in
global differential geometry.
In simple terms, the theorem states that every simple, closed, smooth plane curve
(other than a perfect circle) must have at least four "vertices." arXiv
Here is a breakdown of what that actually means and why it is significant.

1. What is a "Vertex" in this context?
In differential geometry, a vertex is not a sharp corner like on a square. Instead, it
refers to a point of extreme curvature.
Curvature (κ): This measures how sharply the curve bends at any given point.
Mathematics LibreTexts

Vertex: A point where the curvature reaches a local maximum or a local
minimum. Mathematically, it is where the derivative of the curvature with
respect to arc length is zero ( dκ
ds = 0).

2. The Classic Example: The Ellipse
The most intuitive example is a non-circular ellipse.
Two Maxima: The curve is "sharpest" (highest curvature) at the two ends of the
major axis (the "pointy" ends).
Two Minima: The curve is "flattest" (lowest curvature) at the two ends of the
minor axis.
Total: 2 Maxima + 2 Minima = 4 Vertices.

3. Why "Four"?

3. Why "Four"?
You might intuitively think a closed loop only needs two variations in curvature (one
max, one min), but that is topologically impossible for a simple closed curve.
If you start at a point of minimum curvature and move toward a maximum, the
curve bends more sharply.
To close the loop smoothly without crossing itself, the curve must "relax"
(decrease curvature) and then "tighten" again.
The function describing curvature along the loop is periodic. A smooth periodic
function generally oscillates. To return to the start, it must go up and down at
least twice (two peaks and two valleys), resulting in at least four critical points.

4. The Circle Exception
The theorem usually specifies "other than a circle" or notes that a circle has infinite
vertices. arXiv
On a perfect circle, the curvature is constant everywhere (κ = 1/r ).
Therefore, the derivative of curvature is zero everywhere.
Every point on a circle is technically a vertex.

Key Takeaway
The Four Vertex Theorem is a beautiful example of a global property constrained by
local geometry. You cannot draw a smooth, non-intersecting blob that varies in
curvature only twice; the math forces you to have at least four extremes.

What about the inverse four vertex theorem?

You are likely referring to what mathematicians call the Converse to the Four Vertex
Theorem.
While the original theorem says "every valid curve has a certain curvature pattern," the
converse asks the reverse question: "If I invent a curvature pattern, can I build a
valid curve from it?"
The short answer is yes, but it is surprisingly difficult to prove and requires specific
conditions.

The Statement of the Converse
If you have a continuous function defined on a circle (imagine a graph that loops back
on itself) that has:

1.

At least two local minima (totaling 4 "vertices"),

...then there exists a simple, closed plane curve for which this function is the
curvature. arXiv

Why is this harder than the original theorem?
The original theorem is a constraint: "You can't do better than 4." The converse is a
construction challenge: "Build me a curve that matches this specific graph."
When you try to build a curve from a curvature function, you face two major
problems:
1. The "Closing Up" Problem
If you just pick a random function k(s) and start drawing a curve with that curvature,
the curve will almost certainly spiral out or drift away. It won't meet its starting point
to form a closed loop.
To force the curve to close, the function must satisfy strictly balanced integral
conditions (specifically involving sine and cosine weighted integrals).
2. The "Simple" Problem

Even if you get the curve to close, it might cross over itself (like a figure-8). The Four
Vertex Theorem specifically applies to simple (non-intersecting) curves. arXiv
Proving that you can always find a curve that closes and doesn't crash into itself
was a major mathematical hurdle.

The History of the Solution
This wasn't fully solved until relatively recently in mathematical history:
Herman Gluck (1971): Proved the converse for strictly positive curvature. This
means he proved it for convex shapes (shapes that bulge outward everywhere,
like an egg). arXiv
Björn Dahlberg (1997/2005): Proved the full converse. He showed that even if
the curvature goes negative (meaning the curve has dents or concavities), as
long as it has the required 4 vertices and satisfies the closing conditions, a
matching curve exists.

Summary
The Four Vertex Theorem says: "Every simple closed curve oscillates in curvature at
least 4 times."
The Converse says: "Every function that oscillates at least 4 times (and meets closing
conditions) is the curvature of some simple closed curve."

Gluck (1971) had it easy: he assumed strictly positive curvature (k > 0). If curvature
never changes sign, the curve is convex. Convex closed curves are automatically
simple. Wikipedia, the free encycl…
Dahlberg (2005) dropped that condition. He allowed the curve to have dents
(negative curvature). Once you allow dents, you risk the curve crossing over itself (like
a figure-8 or a limaçon with a loop). arXiv
Dahlberg handled this using a powerful combination of Degree Theory (an advanced
version of the "winding number") and an analysis of the "Boundary of Simplicity."
Here is the conceptual breakdown of his argument.

1. The Strategy: Deformation (Homotopy)
Instead of building the curve from scratch, Dahlberg uses a "morphing" argument.
0.

Start with a Circle: We know a perfect circle exists, is simple, and has constant
curvature kcircle .

1.

The Target: We have our desired messy curvature function ktarget (with 4+
vertices).

2.

The Path: We create a smooth path of curvature functions kt that slowly
morphs kcircle into ktarget .

Dahlberg wants to show that as we morph the curvature, we can drag the actual curve

along with it, and—crucially—it will never cross over itself during the process.

2. The Danger Zone: Self-Tangency
Imagine you are morphing a rubber band. It starts as a simple circle. For it to become
non-simple (a figure-8), it must pass through a catastrophic moment where it touches
itself.
This moment is called a Self-Tangency (or a "tacnode").
At this exact moment, two distinct points on the curve touch and share the
same tangent line.
Dahlberg had to prove that, under the constraints of the Four Vertex Theorem, you
can find a path where this disaster never happens.

3. The "Wall" Argument
Think of the Space of All Curves as a giant room.
The "Safe Zone": The middle of the room contains all Simple Curves.
The "Walls": The walls of the room represent curves with Self-Tangencies (the
boundary between simple and non-simple).
The "Outside": The hallway represents non-simple curves (intersecting loops).
Dahlberg defined a map from this room to the Space of Curvature Functions.
He proved that the map behaves like a "covering" (specifically, it has Degree 1).
This means the map is surjective: every curvature function is "hit" by at least one
simple curve.
How did he ensure we don't hit the wall?
He analyzed the geometry of a self-tangency. If a curve touches itself, the curvature at
the touching points must satisfy a very specific relationship (related to the "double
normal" geometric condition).
Dahlberg showed that the set of curvature functions that produce self-tangencies is
"thin" (codimension 1).
Because the degree of the map (the "winding number" of solutions) is 1 for the

Circle, and the map is continuous, the number of simple solutions must remain 1
(algebraically) as long as we don't hit a "wall" that consumes our solution.
He proved that the "Walls" don't separate the Space of Curvatures into
disconnected chunks in a way that would make the solution disappear.
Essentially, you can always wiggle around the dangerous self-intersecting
configurations.

4. The Role of Sturm Oscillation (The "4 Vertices" constraint)
You might ask: "Why does having 4 vertices help avoid intersections?"
Dahlberg (and later refinements) leaned on Sturm Oscillation Theory. This relates the
number of zeros of a function (vertices) to the geometric behavior of the curve.
Low Oscillation: A curve with only 4 vertices is "stiff." It bends, relaxes, bends,
relaxes. It doesn't wiggle enough to easily tie itself into a knot.
High Oscillation: To make a complex self-intersecting knot, you generally need
the curvature to wiggle up and down many times (more vertices).
While you can have a self-intersecting curve with 4 vertices (like a limaçon with a
loop), Dahlberg's degree argument shows that the Simple Curve solution is "stable."
The "Simple Solution" branch doesn't suddenly turn into a "Non-Simple" one because
the 4-vertex condition prevents the kind of wild oscillation needed to "kill" the simple
solution via a homotopy.

Summary
Dahlberg didn't prove that every curve with 4-vertex curvature is simple. He proved
that at least one simple solution must exist.
He did this by showing that:
0.

The "Simple Curve Solution" exists for the circle.

1.

You can deform the circle's curvature to your target curvature.

2.

The topological "count" of simple solutions (Degree) cannot jump from 1 to 0,
because the "Walls of Self-Intersection" are not formidable enough to block the
path for a 4-vertex function.

In our discrete setup (the Flexible Menger Polygon), we have no derivatives.
We are not mapping a circle to a circle via a diffeomorphism.
